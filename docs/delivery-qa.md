# Delivery QA

Use this checklist before calling a memo, workbook, deck, or meeting package ready.

## Evidence QA

- Every important number maps to an evidence ID, source field, or explicit estimate.
- Marketplace, collection date, filter rules, and evidence scale are visible.
- Stale, incomplete, or low-confidence evidence is labeled.
- Raw data is summarized rather than copied into every deliverable.
- No private data is added to the public repository.

## Decision QA

- The output stays inside the current round's allowed scope.
- Product definition appears only after competitor and VOC evidence.
- Confirmed ROI uses Stage 006 inputs, not Stage 003 rough screening.
- Compliance, patent, finance, sourcing, and safety findings remain decision support.
- Facts, signal reading, risks, gaps, and meeting decisions are separated.
- Every blocked status includes a P0 gap, owner or next action, and review trigger.

## Cross-Stage QA

- Product form and target customer do not change silently between stages.
- Keyword, competitor, VOC, feature, and product-definition links remain traceable.
- Package dimensions, FBA assumptions, price, and margin use the same scenario.
- Changed assumptions identify which downstream outputs must be refreshed.
- Physical tests update earlier hypotheses with measured values.

## Deliverable QA

- Headings make the decision context easy to scan.
- Tables use consistent units, currencies, date windows, and confidence labels.
- Missing values stay blank or explicitly pending; they are not replaced with invented numbers.
- Files open correctly and remain editable.
- Spreadsheet formulas show no visible `#REF!`, `#DIV/0!`, `#VALUE!`, `#NAME?`, or `#N/A` errors.
- Slides have no clipped text, overlapping elements, or unreadable evidence notes.

## Executive pressure test

Before a meeting, answer these questions:

1. Why should the reviewer trust the key number?
2. Which claim is a fact, which is an interpretation, and which is still a hypothesis?
3. What changes under conservative, base, and upside scenarios?
4. Which cost, fee, demand, timing, compliance, patent, or sample assumption could reverse the recommendation?
5. What is the strongest competitor counterexample?
6. What evidence is still missing, who owns it, and when will it be reviewed?
7. Are sample tests measurable, with pass/fail criteria and a response to failure?
8. Which questions require an actual meeting decision rather than more analysis?

Record each item as `pass`, `risk`, or `not applicable`. Every `risk` item needs a correction or a visible meeting question.
